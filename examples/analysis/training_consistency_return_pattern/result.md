# Result — Training Consistency and Return Pattern

## Evidence Received

- Six fictional running-activity rows.
- Distance, duration, and training load are present in every row.
- No sleep, fatigue, health, injury, recovery, or subjective fields are present.

## Observed Facts

The consecutive activity gaps are 4, 4, 24, 4, and 5 days. The first and last
three rows compare as follows:

| Segment | Activities | Mean distance | Mean duration | Mean load |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| First three | 3 | 8.33 km | 52.78 min | 81.67 |
| Last three | 3 | 4.00 km | 25.28 min | 37.67 |

The longest represented gap is 24 days. After that gap, the represented
distances increase from 3.0 km to 4.0 km to 5.0 km, and the represented training
load increases from 30 to 38 to 45.

## Interpretation

The synthetic rows show a lower-volume segment after a longer gap, followed by
stepwise increases in the represented distance and load. This pattern organizes
evidence for review; it does not establish why the gap occurred or whether the
pattern is appropriate.

## Decision-Support Options

- **Continue the current pattern:** a human may consider this if relevant
  non-dataset context supports it.
- **Maintain without increasing load:** preserves the recent represented level
  while more observations accumulate.
- **Resume gradually:** uses the lower recent values as a conservative evidence
  baseline, without prescribing a rate.
- **Seek additional context first:** appropriate when subjective condition,
  recovery, medical, or schedule information is material to the decision.

The analysis does not select an option.

## Uncertainty

The cause of the gap is unknown. The sample has no subjective or clinical
context and no evidence about unrecorded activities.

## Unsupported Conclusions

- Injury risk, overtraining, recovery, or readiness.
- That the gap represents injury or a planned break.
- A safe training-load increase.
- Medical or coaching advice.
